Your "whole wheat" bread might not be whole wheat: the brown-color trick

Many "multigrain" or "whole" breads are dyed white flour. Here's how to spot them in 5 seconds.

By CalorIA Scan · July 3, 2026 · 2 min read

Share 𝕏 f ✆

The short version

  • Refining wheat strips out more than half its B vitamins, 90% of its vitamin E, and nearly all the fiber (Harvard).
  • Brown color or the word "multigrain" does NOT guarantee whole grain; the color can come from molasses or caramel.
  • Trick: make sure "100% whole grain / whole-wheat flour" is the first or second ingredient.

Brown doesn't always mean whole

You're at the store, you see a brown, seedy loaf that says "multigrain." Sounds healthy, right? The catch is that color proves nothing: bread can look dark simply because molasses or caramel color was added to a dough made of refined white flour.

"Multigrain" is no guarantee either: it only means several grains are present, not that those grains are whole.

What you lose when grain is refined

Here's what actually matters. According to Harvard's School of Public Health, refining wheat strips away more than half of its B vitamins, 90% of the vitamin E, and virtually all the fiber.

Whole grain, by contrast, keeps that "complete package" of nutrients and fiber. That's why truly whole-grain bread is more filling and better for your digestion and blood sugar.

How to read the package in 5 seconds

Ignore the front and go to the ingredient list. Harvard sums it up: to know a product is 100% whole grain, make sure it's listed first or second in the ingredients ("whole-wheat flour," "whole grain").

Another useful Harvard rule: check the carbohydrate-to-fiber ratio. Healthier products tend to have fewer than 10 g of carbs per gram of fiber. And note: commercial "whole grain" stamps don't, on their own, guarantee the product is healthy.

What to choose

Look for bread that says "100% whole grain" with whole grain as the first ingredient — or better yet, unprocessed grains: oats, brown rice, quinoa. If you track your macros, scan the bread with CalorIA Scan and compare fiber across brands before you decide.
